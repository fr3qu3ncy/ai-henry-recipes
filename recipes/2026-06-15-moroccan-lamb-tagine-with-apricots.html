Moroccan Lamb Tagine with Apricots

Why This Recipe

A completely new cuisine — Moroccan. This slow-cooked lamb tagine is deeply spiced with ras el hanout, studded with sweet apricots, and falls apart at the touch of a fork. It's a proper hands-off meal: 15 minutes of prep, then the oven does the work for nearly 2 hours. Serve over couscous and your kitchen will smell incredible.

Ingredients

  • 500g boneless lamb shoulder, trimmed of excess fat, cut into 3.5cm / 1.5 inch cubes
  • 1 tsp salt, plus more to taste
  • 1/2 tsp black pepper
  • 2 tbsp neutral oil (canola or vegetable)
  • 3 garlic cloves, finely minced
  • 1 large brown onion, diced into 1cm cubes
  • 1 tbsp tomato paste
  • 1 tsp grated fresh ginger
  • 2 cinnamon sticks
  • 3/4 cup chicken stock (low sodium)
  • 1/2 cup dried apricots, whole
  • Zest of 1 lemon
  • 1/2 cup slivered almonds, toasted
  • 1/4 cup fresh coriander / cilantro, roughly chopped
  • Couscous, to serve
  • For the Ras el Hanout Spice Mix:
  • 1 1/2 tsp ground coriander
  • 1 1/2 tsp ground cumin
  • 3/4 tsp ground cardamom
  • 3/4 tsp turmeric powder
  • 1/2 tsp fennel powder (or ground fennel seeds)
  • 1/2 tsp cayenne pepper (reduce to 1/4 tsp for milder)
  • 1/8 tsp ground cloves
  • 1/8 tsp ground ginger
  • 1/4 tsp salt

Instructions

Prep

  • Cut the lamb shoulder into 3.5cm / 1.5 inch cubes, trimming off excess fat.
  • Dice the onion into 1cm cubes. Mince the garlic. Grate the ginger.
  • Mix the ras el hanout spices together in a small bowl: ground coriander, cumin, cardamom, turmeric, fennel powder, cayenne, ground cloves, ground ginger, and salt. Set aside.
  • Toast the slivered almonds in a dry pan over medium heat for 2-3 minutes until golden. Watch carefully — they burn fast. Set aside.
  • Preheat the oven to 180C / 350F (160C fan).

Brown the Lamb

  • Toss the lamb cubes with 1 tsp salt and 1/2 tsp black pepper.
  • Heat the oil in a large oven-proof Dutch oven (or heavy-based casserole dish with a lid) over high heat.
  • Brown the lamb in 2-3 batches — don't crowd the pan. Turn to colour all sides, about 3 minutes per batch. Remove browned lamb to a bowl and repeat.
  • The lamb doesn't need to be cooked through — you just want a deep golden-brown crust on every piece.

Build the Aromatics

  • Turn the heat down to medium-high. Add the diced onion and minced garlic to the same pot. Cook for 3 minutes until soft and translucent.
  • Stir in the tomato paste, grated ginger, cinnamon sticks, and the ras el hanout spice mix.
  • Cook for 1 1/2 minutes, stirring constantly — the spices will become deeply fragrant. This step is key: toasting the spices in the fat unlocks their full flavour.

Slow Cook

  • Pour in the chicken stock and 1/2 cup water. Stir to combine, scraping up any browned bits from the bottom of the pot.
  • Return all the browned lamb to the pot. It should be mostly submerged in the liquid.
  • Bring to a simmer on the stovetop, then cover with the lid.
  • Transfer to the oven and cook for 45 minutes.
  • After 45 minutes, open the oven, add the dried apricots, cover again, and cook for another 1 hour.
  • Halfway through the second hour (around 1 hr 15 min total), check that the sauce hasn't reduced too much — if it looks dry, add 1/4 cup water.
  • The lamb is done when it's fork-tender — a fork should slide in and out with zero resistance. The sauce should be rich, glossy, and deeply coloured.

Finish and Serve

  • Remove from the oven. Discard the cinnamon sticks.
  • Gently stir in the lemon zest — this brightens the rich, spiced sauce beautifully.
  • Taste and adjust seasoning — add a pinch of salt if needed.
  • Prepare couscous according to packet instructions (usually just pour boiling water over, cover for 5 minutes, then fluff with a fork).
  • Spoon the tagine over the couscous, ensuring each portion gets plenty of sauce, apricots, and lamb.
  • Sprinkle generously with toasted almonds and fresh coriander.
  • Serve immediately while hot.

Pro Tips

  • Lamb shoulder is fatty and tough — that's a feature, not a bug. The fat renders down during slow cooking, basting the meat from the inside and creating an incredibly juicy result. Don't substitute a lean cut like leg of lamb; it will dry out.
  • The ras el hanout spice mix is the soul of this dish. If you can't find fennel powder, toast whole fennel seeds in a dry pan for 1 minute then grind them. Make a double batch and keep the rest in an airtight jar — it lasts months.
  • For a shortcut: buy pre-made ras el hanout from the spice aisle and skip making the mix from scratch.
  • Leftovers improve overnight as the flavours meld — the sauce gets even richer. Reheat gently on the stovetop with a splash of water.
