Fesenjan (Persian Pomegranate-Walnut Chicken Stew)

Why This Recipe

Persian cuisine is a rich, unexplored flavor world for Martin — sweet-sour pomegranate molasses, toasted walnuts, and saffron create a deeply complex sauce that braises chicken thighs to fall-off-the-bone tenderness. Most of the 2-hour cook time is hands-off simmering.

Ingredients

  • 3 1/2 cups walnuts
  • 1 tablespoon olive oil
  • 1 medium onion, finely diced
  • 1 teaspoon ground turmeric
  • 8 bone-in chicken thighs, skin removed (about 2 pounds)
  • 1 teaspoon ground cinnamon
  • 1/2 teaspoon saffron threads
  • 3/4 cup pomegranate molasses, plus more as needed
  • 2 tablespoons sugar, plus more as needed
  • Fine sea salt, to taste
  • Black pepper, freshly ground, to taste
  • Pomegranate seeds, for garnish
  • Basmati rice, to serve

Instructions

Prep the Walnuts

  • Finely grind 3 1/2 cups of walnuts in a food processor until they look like coarse sand that clumps together when pressed. Do not over-process — you want ground walnuts, not walnut butter.
  • Place a dry non-stick skillet over medium heat. Add the ground walnuts and toast, stirring continuously for 3-4 minutes until fragrant and lightly golden. Do not let them burn.
  • Pour the toasted ground walnuts into a bowl and set aside.

Cook the Onions

  • Take a large stockpot or Dutch oven with a lid and set over high heat. Add 1 tablespoon olive oil.
  • When the oil glistens, add the finely diced onion. Cook for 5-7 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the onions caramelize and turn golden brown.
  • Add 1 teaspoon turmeric and stir into the onion mixture for 30 seconds until fragrant.

Sear and Simmer the Chicken

  • Add the chicken thighs to the pot and sear for 3-4 minutes on each side until golden brown.
  • Pour in 3 cups of water and add 1 teaspoon cinnamon. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat to low.
  • Cover and simmer for 10 minutes. Remove the chicken thighs and set aside on a plate.

Cook the Walnut Sauce (Key Step)

  • Pour the ground walnuts into the simmering cooking liquid and stir well to combine.
  • Cover the pot and simmer for 1 hour, stirring every 10-15 minutes to prevent the walnuts from sticking to the bottom.
  • After 1 hour, the walnut sauce should have thickened, darkened to a deep golden-brown color (like tahini or honey), and a layer of walnut oil will have formed on top. This is the signature of a proper fesenjan.

Add Pomegranate Molasses and Saffron

  • Bloom the saffron: in a small bowl, add 1 tablespoon hot water. Using a mortar and pestle (or spice grinder), grind 1/2 teaspoon saffron threads to about 1/4 teaspoon ground saffron. Add to the hot water and let steep for 5 minutes.
  • Into the pot with the walnut mixture, add 3/4 cup pomegranate molasses, 2 tablespoons sugar, and the bloomed saffron. Stir well to combine.
  • Taste the sauce and adjust — add more pomegranate molasses for sourness, more sugar for sweetness, or more salt for balance. The ideal fesenjan balances sweet and sour.

Finish the Stew

  • Return the chicken thighs to the stew, nestling them into the sauce.
  • Cover and simmer for 45 minutes until the chicken is falling off the bone and the sauce is a rich, dark brown.
  • A layer of golden walnut oil should form on the surface — this is traditional and delicious. Stir gently, season with salt and pepper to taste, and do a final taste adjustment.

Serve

  • Ladle the fesenjan into a serving dish or individual bowls.
  • Sprinkle with fresh pomegranate seeds for garnish — they add a beautiful pop of color and a burst of tartness.
  • Serve with steamed basmati rice, a dollop of Greek yogurt, and a simple side salad.

Pro Tips

  • The walnut sauce is the soul of fesenjan — do not rush the 1-hour simmer. The walnuts need time to break down, release their oils, and darken to that signature deep brown. If it's still pale after an hour, keep simmering.
  • Sweet-sour balance is everything. Traditional fesenjan is meant to be both sweet and tangy — taste and adjust with more pomegranate molasses (sour) or sugar (sweet) until it hits the right note for your palate.
  • Fesenjan actually improves overnight as the flavors meld and deepen. Make it a day ahead if you can — it tastes even better reheated the next day.
