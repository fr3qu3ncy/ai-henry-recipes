Berbere Braised Lamb Shanks (Ethiopian-Style)

Why This Recipe

Ethiopian berbere spice is a deep, complex blend of chilies, ginger, garlic, and warm spices — completely new territory if you haven't cooked with it. Braised lamb shanks are all about hands-off cooking: sear, braise, and wait while the oven does the work. The meat falls off the bone after 2 hours, smothered in a rich, fragrant sauce. New cuisine, new technique, zero stress.

Ingredients

  • 4 lamb shanks (approx. 600g each)
  • 2 tbsp olive oil
  • 1 large onion, roughly chopped
  • 2 carrots, roughly chopped
  • 3 garlic cloves, left whole
  • 2 tbsp plain flour
  • 1 tbsp tomato purée
  • 2 tbsp berbere spice blend (or see homemade blend below)
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 1 tsp ground coriander
  • 350ml dry white wine
  • 500ml lamb or chicken stock
  • 2 fresh bay leaves
  • Few sprigs fresh rosemary
  • 1 tbsp honey
  • Juice of 1 lemon
  • Fresh parsley or cilantro, to garnish
  • Salt and black pepper, to taste

Instructions

Prep

  • Pat the lamb shanks dry with paper towels — this is key for a good sear.
  • Season generously all over with salt, pepper, and half of the berbere spice blend (1 tbsp).
  • Roughly chop the onion and carrots. Leave garlic cloves whole but peeled.
  • Preheat the oven to 200°C (180°C fan/gas mark 6).
  • If making your own berbere blend: mix 2 tbsp paprika, 1 tbsp ground dried chillies or cayenne, 1 tsp each of ground cumin, coriander, ginger, garlic powder, cinnamon, and cloves, plus a pinch of allspice and fenugreek if you have them.

Sear

  • Heat olive oil in a large casserole dish or ovenproof Dutch oven over high heat on the hob.
  • Sear the lamb shanks for 8-10 minutes total, turning to brown all sides deeply. Don't crowd the pan — work in two batches if needed.
  • Remove the shanks to a plate and set aside. Leave the fond (browned bits) in the pan — that's flavour.

Build the Sauce

  • Reduce heat to medium. Add the chopped onion and carrots to the same pan and cook for 8-10 minutes until starting to soften and colour.
  • Add the whole garlic cloves, rosemary sprigs, and bay leaves. Cook for 2 more minutes until fragrant.
  • Sprinkle the flour over the vegetables and stir for 1 minute to cook out the raw flour taste.
  • Stir in the tomato purée and remaining berbere spice (1 tbsp), plus the cumin and coriander. Cook for 1 minute.
  • Pour in the white wine, scraping up all the browned bits from the bottom of the pan with a wooden spoon.
  • Add the stock and honey. Stir to combine and bring to a gentle simmer.
  • Season well with salt and pepper. Taste — it should be rich and slightly spicy.

Braise

  • Nestle the lamb shanks back into the pan, partially submerged in the sauce.
  • Cover tightly with a lid or heavy foil.
  • Transfer to the preheated oven and braise for 1½-2 hours. The lamb is done when the meat pulls away from the bone easily with a fork — it should be fork-tender, not falling apart completely.
  • Remove from the oven. Carefully lift the shanks out and set aside on a warm plate, tented with foil.

Finish

  • Place the casserole back on the hob over medium heat. Bubble the sauce for 10-15 minutes to reduce and thicken.
  • Strain the sauce through a fine sieve into a jug, pressing on the solids to extract all the flavour. Discard the solids.
  • Stir in the lemon juice and taste — adjust seasoning with more salt, pepper, or a squeeze of lemon if needed.
  • Return the lamb shanks to the sauce to keep warm, or serve immediately.

Serve

  • Spoon the rich berbere sauce over the lamb shanks.
  • Garnish with freshly chopped parsley or cilantro.
  • Serve with creamy mashed potatoes, buttery polenta, or crusty bread to soak up the sauce.
  • The lamb and sauce keep in the fridge for up to 2 days (the flavour actually improves overnight) or freeze for 1 month.

Pro Tips

  • Make-ahead magic: braise the shanks a day ahead, then reheat gently. The flavours deepen and intensify overnight — this is one of those dishes that's genuinely better the next day.
  • Berbere depth: toast your whole spices (cumin seeds, coriander seeds, cloves) in a dry pan for 1-2 minutes before grinding — it releases essential oils and makes a massive difference to the spice blend's flavour.
