Japanese Meat and Potato Stew (Nikujaga)

Why This Recipe

A beloved Japanese home-cooking classic — beef and potatoes simmered in a sweet-savory dashi-soy broth. It's the taste of 'mum's cooking' in Japan, and a perfect introduction to Japanese comfort food that's quick, one-pan, and deeply flavorful.

Ingredients

  • 200g thinly sliced beef (or pork)
  • 2 large potatoes (about 600g), cut into 3-4cm cubes
  • 120g onion, sliced into 1cm wide strips
  • 60g carrot, thinly sliced then cut in halves or quarters
  • 100g shirataki (konnyaku yum noodles), cut into 10cm strands
  • 1 tbsp vegetable oil
  • 350ml dashi stock
  • 2 tbsp cooking sake
  • 1/2 tbsp mirin
  • 2 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1 tbsp sugar
  • 50g green beans, blanched and cut into 3cm lengths

Instructions

Prep

  • Cut beef slices into large bite-size pieces if they're large.
  • Peel and cube potatoes into 3-4cm pieces.
  • Slice onion into 1cm wide strips.
  • Thinly slice carrot, then cut into halves or quarters.
  • Cut shirataki noodles into 10cm strands and rinse under cold water.
  • Blanch green beans in boiling water for 1 minute, then drain and cut into 3cm lengths.

Cook

  • Heat oil in a saucepan over medium-high heat.
  • Add beef and sauté, breaking pieces apart, until the meat turns dark brown.
  • Add potatoes, onion, carrot, and shirataki. Cook for 1 minute, ensuring all vegetables are coated with oil.
  • Pour in dashi stock and bring to a boil.
  • Skim off any scum from the surface using a ladle.
  • Add sake, mirin, and sugar. Stir to dissolve.
  • Once boiling again, reduce heat to low. Add soy sauce.
  • Place a drop lid (or a piece of parchment paper) on the surface.
  • Simmer gently for about 10 minutes until potatoes are tender — test by piercing with a thin skewer; it should slide through easily.

Serve

  • Transfer the stew to a serving bowl or individual plates.
  • Scatter the blanched green beans over the top for color and freshness.
  • Serve immediately while hot, ideally with steamed rice.

Pro Tips

  • If you can't find dashi stock, dissolve a dashi granule (Hondashi) in hot water, or substitute with a mix of water + a pinch of kombu + bonito flakes for 5 minutes.
  • A drop lid (otoshibuta) keeps the simmering liquid from reducing too fast and ensures even cooking. No drop lid? Cut a circle of parchment paper slightly smaller than your pan — it works perfectly.
  • Nikujaga is traditionally beef in most of Japan, but pork is common in Okinawa. Use whichever you prefer — the technique is identical.
