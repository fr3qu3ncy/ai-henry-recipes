Vietnamese Grilled Lemongrass Pork (Thit Heo Nuong Xa)

Why This Recipe

Vietnamese cuisine is a completely new exploration for Martin — this iconic grilled pork uses a fragrant lemongrass-fish sauce marinade that transforms humble pork shoulder into something restaurant-quality. Quick 8-minute grill time, versatile enough for rice plates, banh mi, or bun bowls.

Ingredients

  • 1 ⅓ lbs (600g) boneless pork shoulder steak
  • 1 stalk lemongrass, trimmed and finely chopped (about 3 tablespoons)
  • 1 tablespoon chopped shallot or yellow onion
  • 1 tablespoon chopped garlic
  • ¼ teaspoon freshly ground black pepper
  • 1½ to 2 tablespoons light brown sugar, palm sugar, or granulated sugar
  • 1½ teaspoons dark soy sauce (or mix regular soy sauce with a touch of molasses)
  • 1½ tablespoons fish sauce
  • 1 tablespoon neutral oil (vegetable or canola)

Instructions

Prep the Pork

  • Cut the pork shoulder steak into pieces about 3 to 4 inches wide and roughly ½ inch thick — each piece should be no bigger than your hand.
  • If the pork is very thick, you can slice it horizontally to achieve the right thickness. Set the cut pork aside.

Make the Marinade

  • Put the chopped lemongrass, shallot, garlic, black pepper, and sugar into a small food processor.
  • Add the dark soy sauce, fish sauce, and neutral oil.
  • Process until relatively smooth — the marinade should be a rich chocolate brown color.
  • If you don't have a food processor, finely mince the lemongrass, shallot, and garlic by hand in a bowl, then stir in the sugar, soy sauce, fish sauce, and oil until well combined.
  • Scrape the marinade into a medium bowl.

Marinate

  • Add the pork pieces to the marinade and massage well with your hands to coat every piece thoroughly.
  • Cover and let marinate at room temperature for 30 minutes (minimum).
  • For deeper flavor, refrigerate for up to 24 hours — if doing so, let the meat sit out for 45 minutes before grilling to take the chill off.
  • The marinade penetrates deeply into the pork, so even 30 minutes makes a real difference.

Grill

  • Preheat your grill to medium-high heat (about 375-400°F / 190-200°C).
  • Remove pork from marinade, shaking off excess. Discard the used marinade.
  • Grill the pork pieces for 6 to 8 minutes total, turning frequently to cook evenly on all sides.
  • The pork should develop a deep caramelized exterior with slight char marks — this is where the flavor lives.
  • Test doneness by nicking the thickest piece with a knife — juices should run clear, no pink.
  • If using a stovetop grill pan instead of an outdoor grill, cook over medium-high heat for the same time, turning every 1-2 minutes.

Rest & Serve

  • Transfer the grilled pork to a clean plate and let rest for 10 minutes — this lets the juices redistribute for maximum tenderness.
  • Slice the pork into thin strips — this is the traditional Vietnamese approach, making the meat easier to pick up with chopsticks.
  • Serve with steamed jasmine rice, a simple stir-fried vegetable, and pickled daikon and carrots (do chua) if you have them.
  • Leftovers are fantastic in banh mi sandwiches or tossed into bun rice noodle bowls.

Pro Tips

  • The marinade is incredibly versatile — use the same lemongrass-fish sauce base with chicken thighs, pork belly, or even shrimp for skewers. Just adjust cook times.
  • No fresh lemongrass? Use 1½ teaspoons of ground lemongrass (Burlap & Barrel's 'drumstick lemongrass' from Vietnam is excellent), or substitute ½ teaspoon Chinese five-spice powder for a different but equally aromatic profile.
  • Bringing the pork to room temperature before grilling is a game-changer — cold meat straight from the fridge seizes up and cooks unevenly. The 45-minute sit-out makes the difference between juicy and dry.
