Jjajangmyeon (Korean Black Bean Noodles)

Why This Recipe

Korean black bean noodles introduce a completely new technique — stir-frying fermented black bean paste (chunjang) to unlock deep, savory umami. It's Martin's Korean cuisine exploration continued, with a dish that's quick (30-40 min), comforting, and wildly different from anything he's cooked before.

Ingredients

  • 1kg fresh jajangmyeon noodles (or udon/Kalguksu noodles)
  • 600g diced pork belly or pork shoulder
  • 1 Tbsp rice wine (or mirin)
  • 5 sprinkles ground salt
  • 5 sprinkles ground black pepper
  • 1/4 tsp ginger powder
  • 1 large onion (150g), cut into small cubes
  • 1 medium zucchini (110g), cut into small cubes
  • 2 large potatoes (470g), cut into small cubes
  • 7 large button mushrooms (240g), thinly sliced
  • 1/4 small cabbage (160g), cut into small-medium pieces
  • 6 Tbsp Korean black bean paste (Chunjang)
  • 6 Tbsp lard (or rice bran/cooking oil)
  • 2 Tbsp brown sugar
  • 4 Tbsp rice wine (or mirin)
  • 1 cup chicken stock
  • 1 cup water
  • Slurry: 5 Tbsp potato starch + 4 Tbsp water
  • 1 large cucumber, julienned (for garnish)
  • Frozen peas or sweet corn (optional, for garnish)
  • Hard boiled egg (optional, for garnish)

Instructions

Prep

  • Rinse the diced pork in cold water, pat dry with kitchen paper. Toss with 1 Tbsp rice wine, salt, black pepper, and ginger powder. Mix well and marinate for 15 minutes.
  • Cut the onion, zucchini, and potato into small uniform cubes (about 1cm).
  • Thinly slice the mushrooms.
  • Cut the cabbage into small to medium-sized pieces.
  • Julienne the cucumber for garnish. Set aside.

Fry the Black Bean Paste

  • Preheat a wok or large deep skillet until the bottom is well heated over medium heat.
  • Add the lard (or cooking oil) and let it melt. Add the Korean black bean paste (Chunjang) and stir constantly for 3-5 minutes. This step is critical — frying the paste unlocks its deep, savory flavour. Do not let it burn.
  • Add the brown sugar and stir for an additional 2-3 minutes until the paste is fragrant and glossy.
  • Scoop out the fried black bean paste (without the oil) and set aside. Leave the oil in the wok for the next step.

Cook the Pork and Vegetables

  • Add the marinated pork to the wok and stir-fry until half cooked (about 3-4 minutes).
  • Add the onion, zucchini, and potato cubes. Stir-fry for 3-5 minutes until vegetables are about 1/3 cooked.
  • Add the sliced mushrooms and cabbage pieces. Stir-fry for 2-3 minutes more.
  • Return the fried black bean paste to the wok and mix thoroughly with the vegetables and pork. Stir for 1-2 minutes to coat everything.
  • Pour in the chicken stock, water, and 4 Tbsp rice wine. Simmer for 5-7 minutes on medium heat. Cover with a lid if possible to speed up cooking. The sauce should be thick and glossy, the vegetables tender but not mushy.

Thicken the Sauce

  • Mix the potato starch and water to make a slurry. Pour it into the wok while stirring constantly.
  • Stir for 30-60 seconds — the sauce will thicken noticeably. This is the final stage of making the black bean sauce. If it looks too thick, add a splash of water to loosen it.

Cook the Noodles

  • While the sauce is simmering, bring a large pot of water to a boil.
  • Add the noodles and boil for 3-5 minutes until cooked through (follow package instructions if using udon or Kalguksu).
  • Drain the noodles and rinse thoroughly under cold running water to remove excess starch. Drain again well.
  • Place the noodles in serving bowls.

Serve

  • Ladle a generous portion of the black bean sauce over the noodles in each bowl.
  • Garnish with julienned cucumber, frozen peas or sweet corn, and a halved hard-boiled egg if desired.
  • Mix the sauce and noodles thoroughly with chopsticks or a fork before eating — the sauce should coat every strand.
  • Serve immediately with pickled radish on the side (traditional and aids digestion). Eat quickly before the noodles absorb too much sauce and swell.

Pro Tips

  • Frying the Chunjang (black bean paste) in oil before adding anything else is the most important step — it transforms the paste from bland to deeply aromatic and unlocks the umami. Do not skip this, and do not let it burn.
  • Use potato starch for the slurry, not cornstarch — potato starch gives the sauce a glossier, more authentic texture. If you can't find potato starch, cornstarch works as a substitute but the sauce will be slightly less shiny.
  • Chunjang is very salty — taste the sauce before adding extra salt. The fried paste + pork should provide enough seasoning on their own.
